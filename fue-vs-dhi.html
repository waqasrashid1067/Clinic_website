FUE or DHI:
which one is right for you?

Both techniques take follicles out one at a time. The difference is what happens in the seconds after, and that changes who each one suits.

Asim Shahzad MAX FAX Clinic · 1 July 2026 · 8 min read

On this page

  1. What the two have in common
  2. How FUE works
  3. How DHI works
  4. Which one suits which patient
  5. Why DHI usually costs more
  6. The part nobody advertises

What the two have in common

Both FUE and DHI are follicular unit extraction. In both, a small rotary punch — usually between 0.7 and 0.9 mm — removes individual follicular units from the back and sides of the scalp, where hair is genetically resistant to thinning. Neither one cuts a strip, and neither one leaves a linear scar.

So when a clinic markets DHI as an alternative to FUE, that is not quite right. DHI is a placement method. The extraction stage is the same operation.

How FUE works

After extraction, the grafts are sorted under magnification into ones, twos and threes, and kept in chilled holding solution. The surgeon then opens the recipient sites — tiny slits, each one angled to match how hair grew there originally — across the whole area. Only once every channel is open does placement begin.

Opening the channels as a separate stage has one real advantage: the surgeon can see the entire design at once, and adjust density and direction before a single graft goes in. It is easier to keep a hairline irregular in the way real hairlines are irregular.

How DHI works

In DHI the graft is loaded into a hollow implanter pen — Choi-type, typically 0.8 to 1.0 mm. Pressing the plunger opens the channel and delivers the follicle in the same movement. There is no separate channel-opening stage.

Two things follow from that:

  • Shorter time out of the body. Follicles survive better the less time they spend outside the scalp, and the pen shortens that window.
  • Work between existing hairs. Because the channel is created and filled instantly, there is less risk of damaging hairs you still have. That matters when you are adding density rather than covering bare scalp.

Which one suits which patient

There is no universally better technique, only a better fit. Broadly:

  • Large bald areas, high graft counts. FUE. It is faster across a big surface, and speed matters when you are placing three thousand grafts in one sitting.
  • Hairline design and small dense zones. DHI. Tighter packing, finer control over angle at the front row.
  • Thinning rather than baldness. DHI, usually, because you are threading between hairs that are still there.
  • Beard and eyebrows. DHI, almost always. Facial hair sits at an extreme angle to the skin and the pen gives more control over direction.
  • Budget is the constraint. FUE. Well-executed FUE beats poorly rushed DHI every time.

Many sessions use both: DHI along the hairline where precision counts most, FUE behind it where volume counts. That is not a compromise, it is the sensible answer.

Why DHI usually costs more

Implanter pen tips are single-use, and a full session gets through a lot of them. The technique is also slower per graft and needs a larger trained team loading pens continuously. The higher price is labour and consumables, not a better outcome by default.

The part nobody advertises

The technique matters far less than three things almost no advertisement mentions:

  1. Your donor supply. It is finite. A surgeon who empties it at thirty has nothing left when you are forty-five and the pattern has moved back.
  2. Hairline design. A hairline that suits your face at twenty-five can look wrong at fifty. It should be drawn for the face you will have, not the one you miss.
  3. Who is actually holding the punch. In many high-volume clinics the surgeon designs the hairline and technicians do the rest. Ask directly. It is a fair question and you deserve a straight answer.

If you want to know which technique fits your case, that is what the consultation is for. It is free, it takes about forty minutes, and you leave with a written plan rather than a booking.
